# Style Toolkit

Use these as small editorial tools. Select them deliberately; do not turn the draft into a demonstration of techniques.

Each tool has:

```text
Use when | Input | Operation | Output shape | Avoid
```

## STY-01 Concrete noun + active verb

**Use when:** A sentence relies on abstract nouns, evaluations, or weak copulas.

**Input:** An abstract claim plus observable evidence.

**Operation:** Name the actor, action, object, time, and consequence.

**Output shape:**

```text
[Actor] [active verb] [specific object], causing [observable consequence].
```

**Avoid:** Pretending a motive is observable.

## STY-02 Freight-train sentence

A "freight-train sentence" uses one clear subject–verb engine to pull several logically parallel cars.

**Use when:** Several related actions, consequences, or examples belong to one movement and breaking them apart would feel choppy.

**Input:** One stable subject and 2–5 parallel elements.

**Operation:** Put the subject and verb early; attach parallel clauses or phrases in a consistent grammatical form.

**Output shape:**

```text
The rule shortened review time, shifted decisions to managers, and left contractors outside the appeal process.
```

**Avoid:** More cars than the reader can track; nonparallel items; burying the main verb.

## STY-03 Hook-on clause

**Use when:** A static explanation, quote, or description interrupts action.

**Input:** An active main clause plus necessary context.

**Operation:** Attach the context to the moving sentence through a relative, participial, appositive, or subordinate clause.

**Output shape:**

```text
She closed the recorder, a step the company policy allowed but her manager discouraged.
```

**Avoid:** Dangling modifiers and long chains of hooks.

## STY-04 Parallel structure

**Use when:** Comparing actors, steps, costs, choices, or consequences.

**Input:** Items that are truly comparable.

**Operation:** Use the same grammatical shape and similar information depth.

**Output shape:**

```text
Managers wanted a searchable record; employees wanted room to think aloud.
```

**Avoid:** Forced symmetry, rule-of-three padding, or false equivalence.

## STY-05 Keyword placement

**Use when:** One word or phrase carries the sentence's force.

**Input:** A sentence with a buried key idea.

**Operation:** Move the key phrase to the beginning for framing or the end for impact.

**Output shape:**

```text
What the archive preserved was not only decisions. It preserved hesitation.
```

**Avoid:** Manufactured drama or withholding basic clarity.

## STY-06 Controlled repetition

**Use when:** A term is precise, thematic, or structurally useful.

**Input:** One exact keyword.

**Operation:** Repeat it at deliberate intervals instead of cycling through synonyms.

**Output shape:**

```text
The company called it memory. Employees called it a record. Lawyers called it discoverable.
```

**Avoid:** Slogan rhythm, excessive anaphora, or repetition without development.

## STY-07 Short-force sentence

**Use when:** A verified conclusion, turn, or consequence deserves emphasis.

**Input:** One important idea.

**Operation:** Remove subordinate material and give the idea an independent sentence.

**Output shape:**

```text
The exception became the rule.
```

**Avoid:** Staccato strings, fake punchlines, and unsupported aphorisms.

## STY-08 Long/short rhythm variation

**Use when:** Paragraphs sound mechanically uniform.

**Input:** A sequence of sentences with similar length or syntax.

**Operation:** Let complex explanation use a longer controlled sentence, then land the consequence in a shorter one.

**Output:** Deliberate changes in reading speed.

**Avoid:** Random variation that breaks logical grouping.

## STY-09 Action motion

**Use when:** People appear only as quoted heads or the subject lacks natural plot.

**Input:** Documented actions, sequence, and consequence.

**Operation:** Put decisions, gestures, work, travel, attempts, failures, and responses into the paragraph.

**Output shape:**

```text
He opened the settings, disabled retention, and sent the policy to his team. The next meeting still recorded by default.
```

**Avoid:** Trivial gestures used as cinematic decoration.

## STY-10 Macro/micro alternation

**Use when:** The draft stays abstract too long or remains trapped in one anecdote.

**Input:** Aggregate evidence plus a representative person/place/action.

**Operation:** Alternate scale and instance:

```text
pattern → case → meaning → pattern
```

**Avoid:** Claiming the case proves prevalence.

## STY-11 Contrast braid

**Use when:** Opposing forces create the story's movement.

**Input:** Actions from both sides and a shared decision or resource.

**Operation:** Alternate action and consequence, not opinion and opinion.

**Output shape:**

```text
A acts → B absorbs consequence → B responds → A changes course
```

**Avoid:** False balance and equal paragraph space as a substitute for evidence.

## STY-12 Controlled digression and return

**Use when:** The reader needs history, mechanism, or context before the live action can continue.

**Input:** A present trigger and a necessary aside.

**Operation:** Enter through a concrete question and return through a concrete action or consequence.

**Output shape:**

```text
present action → brief necessary context → present consequence
```

**Avoid:** Background that becomes a second article.

## STY-13 Data humanization

**Use when:** A number is necessary but hard to interpret.

**Input:** Verified data with denominator, time window, and method.

**Operation:** Prefer rates, change over time, per-person values, or a valid familiar comparison.

**Output shape:**

```text
The share rose from 18% to 31% in two years, roughly one additional worker in every eight.
```

**Avoid:** Cute but misleading analogies, omitted uncertainty, or false precision.

## STY-14 Quote punch

**Use when:** A source's exact words add authority, emotion, voice, or a decisive formulation.

**Input:** Verified quotation and locator.

**Operation:** Paraphrase routine setup; quote only the irreplaceable core.

**Output shape:**

```text
She described the system as "a memory nobody can leave."
```

**Avoid:** Quote fragments stitched into the writer's sentence, composite quotes, or cleanup that changes meaning.

## STY-15 Scene engine

**Use when:** A documented sequence embodies the theme.

**Input:** Time/place, actors, observable action, sequence, consequence, sourcing basis.

**Operation:** Write in event order and include only details with evidentiary or character function.

**Output:** A scene that changes the story.

**Avoid:** Weather, décor, facial expressions, inner thoughts, or dialogue not supported by observation or attributed recollection.

## STY-16 Transition by actual movement

**Use when:** A section change feels announced rather than earned.

**Input:** The real relationship between adjacent sections.

**Operation:** Name the change in time, place, actor, cause, consequence, contradiction, or reaction.

**Output shape:**

```text
The archive saved managers time. It also moved sensitive conversations elsewhere.
```

**Avoid:** "Another aspect," "on the other hand," or generic signposting without content.

## STY-17 Conversational reader calibration

**Use when:** The prose is correct but remote or overexplained.

**Input:** A specific intelligent reader and their likely knowledge.

**Operation:** Explain only what that reader needs now; use ordinary words where precise; allow measured directness.

**Output:** Prose that feels addressed rather than broadcast.

**Avoid:** Fake intimacy, rhetorical questions, slang inconsistent with the author, or chatbot phrases.

## STY-18 Echo ending

**Use when:** The opening image, action, person, or phrase has acquired deeper meaning.

**Input:** Opening element plus later development.

**Operation:** Return without repeating the opening verbatim; show changed meaning.

**Avoid:** A neat circle unsupported by real action.

## STY-19 Forward-motion ending

**Use when:** The process continues and a reported next action or deadline exists.

**Input:** Actor, committed action, obstacle, date or condition.

**Operation:** End on what someone will do next, with uncertainty bounded.

**Avoid:** Generic "only time will tell."

## STY-20 Widening-frame ending

**Use when:** A specific story has earned a broader significance through the body.

**Input:** Repeated evidence connecting case and larger pattern.

**Operation:** Widen one level, not five; keep the final statement evidence-based.

**Avoid:** Sudden moral, civilization-scale claim, or new thesis.

## STY-21 Precision through limited break

**Use when:** A metaphor, analogy, or unusual phrasing makes a real relation visible.

**Input:** An exact observable relation.

**Operation:** Use one controlled image, then return to literal reporting.

**Avoid:** Decorative metaphor, inflated symbolism, or images that imply facts not established.

## STY-22 Paragraph locomotive

**Use when:** A paragraph stalls in explanation.

**Input:** One movement or question.

**Operation:**

```text
specific action/fact
→ meaning
→ evidence/attribution
→ consequence
```

Keep one dominant subject or causal movement.

**Avoid:** Topic sentences that merely label a category.

## Choosing tools by problem

| Problem | First tool to try |
|---|---|
| abstract, bureaucratic prose | STY-01 |
| choppy list of related facts | STY-02 |
| explanation stops action | STY-03 |
| comparison is muddy | STY-04 |
| important idea feels buried | STY-05 |
| synonym cycling | STY-06 |
| conclusion lacks force | STY-07 |
| robotic cadence | STY-08 |
| talking heads | STY-09 |
| too abstract / too anecdotal | STY-10 |
| conflict is just quotes | STY-11 |
| background sprawls | STY-12 |
| numbers feel inert | STY-13 |
| quotes repeat facts | STY-14 |
| scene feels decorative | STY-15 |
| transition feels generic | STY-16 |
| prose feels distant | STY-17 |
| opening can return with new meaning | STY-18 |
| live process has next action | STY-19 |
| broader meaning is earned | STY-20 |
| literal prose cannot show relation clearly | STY-21 |
| paragraph has no movement | STY-22 |

## Final restraint test

For every noticeable technique, ask:

```text
Does the material require this?
Does it improve accuracy or movement?
Would a plain sentence work better?
```

When in doubt, choose the plain sentence.
