# Section Craft

Use this reference when drafting or revising specific article components.

## Lead

### Hard-news lead

Use when the event itself is urgent and consequential.

```text
[Actor] [did/announced/changed] [specific action] on [time],
affecting [who/how].
```

Clarity beats suspense.

### Summary lead

Use when the core development is more important than one scene.

A good summary lead contains a concrete actor, change, and consequence while leaving a meaningful "how" or "why" open.

### Anecdotal lead

Use a short scene only when it is:

- true;
- representative;
- understandable without heavy context;
- intrinsically interesting;
- directly connected to the theme.

After the anecdote, move quickly to the nut graf. Do not make the reader guess for several paragraphs why the scene matters.

### Quotation lead

Use rarely. The line must be distinctive, intelligible, and central before attribution is explained.

### Descriptive lead

Use when place or physical arrangement is evidence. Avoid generic weather, décor, and cinematic staging.

## Nut graf

A reliable pattern:

```text
What is happening:
Why now:
Who is affected:
What tension or question the article will pursue:
Why the reporting matters:
```

The nut graf is not an abstract essay thesis. It should point to observable facts and actions.

## Paragraph and section movement

A useful paragraph sequence:

```text
specific fact or action
→ meaning
→ evidence or attribution
→ consequence
```

A useful section sequence:

```text
claim
→ data/document
→ person/action
→ mechanism
→ counterpoint
→ next development
```

Do not repeat the same claim through three quotations.

## Data

### Selection test

Keep a number only if it changes the reader's understanding of:

- scale;
- trend;
- comparison;
- probability;
- cost;
- risk;
- distribution.

### Presentation

Prefer:

- "rose from 18% to 31%" over two disconnected totals;
- "about one in three" when precision is not the story;
- per-person or per-unit figures when totals obscure meaning;
- a familiar comparison when mathematically and conceptually valid.

Always preserve:

- time window;
- population;
- denominator;
- method;
- uncertainty when material.

## Quotations

Use direct quotation for voice, emotion, authority, or a decisive formulation.

Use paraphrase for:

- routine fact;
- chronology;
- technical explanation;
- long, repetitive answers;
- language requiring heavy cleanup.

Do not create composite quotations. Ellipses and brackets must not alter meaning.

## Character

Feature a small number of memorable people.

Introduce with only the details needed to understand:

- role;
- stake;
- action;
- relevant contradiction.

Return to a character because the story develops, not merely to remind the reader they exist.

## Scene

A scene needs:

```text
time/place
actors
observable action
sequence
consequence
```

Use only details supported by observation, records, or attributed recollection. Mark reconstructed scenes and explain their basis when appropriate.

## Description

Choose details that:

- prove a fact;
- reveal a person;
- show a system in action;
- create a meaningful contrast.

Prefer exact nouns and active verbs to evaluative adjectives.

Weak:

```text
The chaotic office was extremely tense.
```

Stronger:

```text
Three managers stood behind one laptop while the support queue climbed past 600.
```

## Attribution

Use full identification when identity affects authority, interest, or credibility.

Avoid interrupting every sentence with redundant attribution. But do not hide the source of disputed, interested, or non-obvious claims.

## Transitions

Transition by naming the actual movement:

- time;
- place;
- actor;
- cause;
- consequence;
- contradiction;
- reaction.

Weak:

```text
There is another side to the issue.
```

Stronger:

```text
The same archive that helped managers recover decisions made employees move sensitive conversations off the platform.
```

## Ending

### Echo

Return to the opening element after the reader understands its larger meaning.

### Forward movement

End with the next decision, action, deadline, or risk already supported by reporting.

### Widening frame

Move from the specific case to a broader significance established throughout the article.

Avoid:

- a new decisive fact;
- an unearned moral;
- a generic quotation;
- a prediction presented as certainty;
- a summary of everything already said.

## Headline and deck

Headline:

- name the central change or tension;
- use precise verbs;
- avoid claims stronger than the article.

Deck:

- add consequence, actor, or context;
- do not merely repeat the headline;
- reveal enough to attract the right reader, not every reader.
