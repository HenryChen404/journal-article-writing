# Deep Research Workflow

Use this reference when the assignment requires substantial external research, source discovery, or evidence synthesis.

## 1. Start with a reporting question, not a search term

Translate a broad topic into a provisional dynamic:

```text
development → impact → reaction
```

Example:

```text
Broad topic: AI meeting assistants

Development:
Companies are making meeting transcription the default.

Impact:
Informal conversation becomes searchable organizational data.

Reaction:
Employees change behavior while companies create new retention and consent rules.
```

This dynamic determines what evidence to seek.

## 2. Build the six-box question tree

### History

- What happened before the visible change?
- Which earlier policy, technology, market condition, or institution made it possible?
- Which timeline points are genuinely causal rather than merely adjacent?

### Scope

- How large is the phenomenon?
- Where does it occur, and where does it not?
- Which populations, institutions, geographies, and time windows are included?
- What denominator makes the scale meaningful?

### Causes

- What mechanisms are proposed?
- Which actors benefit from each explanation?
- What competing explanations exist?
- Which evidence would distinguish them?

### Impacts

- Who gains, loses, adapts, or becomes constrained?
- What changes in behavior, incentives, cost, time, power, or risk?
- Which impacts are measured and which are merely predicted?

### Reactions

- Who is responding?
- Are they promoting, resisting, regulating, evading, compensating, or redesigning?
- What action can be directly observed or documented?

### Future

- Which next steps are already committed?
- Which outcomes are conditional?
- What would invalidate the forecast?

Do not allocate equal space to all six boxes. The story hypothesis determines priority.

## 3. Use a source-role ladder

### Primary records

Examples:

- laws, regulations, court records;
- filings, budgets, contracts, meeting minutes;
- company documentation, technical papers, product logs;
- datasets, surveys, official statistics;
- contemporaneous emails, memos, transcripts, photos, and recordings.

Best for: dates, decisions, definitions, mechanisms, and scale.

### Direct participants

Examples:

- decision-makers;
- workers or users carrying out the action;
- people directly affected;
- witnesses;
- opponents and implementers.

Best for: action, sequence, consequence, scene, motive claims, and lived experience.

### Independent interpreters

Examples:

- domain researchers;
- historians;
- lawyers;
- statisticians;
- technical specialists;
- experienced practitioners without a direct stake.

Best for: mechanism, comparison, uncertainty, and challenge.

### Interested advocates

Examples:

- companies, campaigns, trade groups, unions, activist organizations;
- consultants selling a relevant service;
- political offices.

Best for: positions, strategy, records they uniquely hold, and leads. Treat interpretation as interested rather than neutral.

### Secondary reporting

Best for: orientation, chronology, source discovery, and identifying disputed claims. Trace important claims back to primary or direct evidence when possible.

## 4. Design searches in passes

### Landscape pass

Search for:

- the central phenomenon and its synonyms;
- timeline, policy, market, technical, and geographic variants;
- key actors and institutions;
- datasets and primary records;
- major disputes and known failures.

Output: actor map, vocabulary map, timeline, and primary-source list.

### Hypothesis pass

Break the provisional hypothesis into clauses. Search each clause separately.

```text
Clause: default transcription changes employee speech behavior
Search families:
- meeting transcription workplace behavior study
- employee consent AI notetaker policy
- meeting recording chilling effect workplace
- company AI meeting assistant retention policy
```

Output: evidence for, evidence against, and unresolved clauses.

### Contradiction pass

Use adversarial search terms:

```text
criticism
limitations
failed
no evidence
overstated
retracted
lawsuit
complaint
counterexample
independent audit
methodology
sample bias
```

Also search for places where the supposed trend did not appear.

### Gap pass

Search only unresolved items that affect:

- the central claim;
- fairness;
- chronology;
- scale;
- causality;
- a critical scene or example;
- the ending.

## 5. Build an evidence matrix

Use rows for claims and columns for evidence roles.

| Claim | Document/data | Direct participant | Independent corroboration | Counterevidence | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Default recording is increasing | deployment data | admins/users | analyst | firms rejecting it | provisional |
| Recording changes behavior | policy/complaints | employees | researcher | no-effect studies | weak |
| Firms are reacting | policy revisions | executives | lawyer | no enforcement | supported |

A major claim is strongest when evidence types are complementary rather than repetitive.

## 6. Capture source records

For every source, store:

```text
ID:
Title:
Author / organization:
Date:
URL / path:
Accessed:
Source type:
Source role:
Claims supported:
Key evidence:
Caveats:
Conflicts of interest:
Contradictions:
Intended section:
```

Keep quotations short and necessary. Record page, timestamp, table, or section identifiers.

## 7. Interview planning

Prepare separate question sets by role.

### Direct participant

Ask for:

- a specific recent instance;
- sequence: what happened first, next, and after;
- exact decisions and alternatives;
- sensory or environmental detail they directly observed;
- documents, messages, screenshots, or records;
- what changed compared with before;
- what the person did in response.

### Decision-maker

Ask for:

- authority and responsibility;
- goals, constraints, tradeoffs, and metrics;
- implementation details;
- known adverse effects;
- exceptions and failures;
- what evidence would cause a policy change.

### Expert

Ask for:

- mechanism;
- strongest evidence;
- limits and alternative explanations;
- what journalists commonly misunderstand;
- which direct actors and primary records to pursue.

Do not use expert interpretation as a substitute for direct reporting.

## 8. Use a reporting stop rule

Research is sufficiently mature when:

- the story hypothesis has survived meaningful attempts to disprove it;
- each major claim has traceable support;
- critical actors have been represented or their nonresponse is documented;
- evidence includes more than one role and one source type;
- chronology and scale are clear;
- remaining gaps can be stated without misleading the reader;
- new research mostly repeats existing findings.

Stop because the evidentiary threshold is met, not because a preferred conclusion feels complete.

## 9. Required research outputs

For substantial assignments, produce:

1. revised story hypothesis;
2. research dossier;
3. source map;
4. evidence matrix;
5. contradiction log;
6. missing-evidence and interview list;
7. proposed article structure.
