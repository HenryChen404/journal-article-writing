# Story Hypothesis and Scope

Use this reference to turn a subject into a testable story.

## Topic, angle, and hypothesis are different

```text
Topic:
AI in the workplace

Angle:
The tension between organizational memory and employee candor

Story hypothesis:
As companies make AI meeting transcription the default, informal speech becomes
searchable organizational data. Employees are changing what they say and where
they say it, while managers struggle to balance knowledge capture with trust.
```

A topic names a field. An angle selects a relationship. A hypothesis predicts a reportable dynamic.

## The seven-part hypothesis

Fill in:

```text
Central development:
Maturity stage:
Most important impact:
Affected people / human center:
Reaction:
Opposing force or constraint:
Out of scope:
```

### Central development

Use an actor and a verb.

Weak:

```text
Remote work and culture
```

Stronger:

```text
Several large employers are replacing flexible remote arrangements with
team-level office attendance requirements.
```

### Maturity stage

- **Development stage**: the change is new; direct effects are limited or unclear.
- **Impact stage**: concrete consequences have appeared.
- **Reaction stage**: affected actors are adapting, resisting, exploiting, or regulating.

A mature story usually earns more space for impact and reaction than for re-explaining the original development.

### Impact

Choose the consequence that is:

- important;
- specific;
- evidenced;
- embodied by people or institutions;
- not already exhausted by prior coverage.

### Human center

A person is not decoration. Select people whose actions or consequences reveal the mechanism.

### Conflict

Conflict can be:

- goal versus goal;
- promise versus implementation;
- public value versus private incentive;
- tradition versus change;
- person versus institution;
- speed versus accuracy;
- efficiency versus autonomy;
- local experience versus aggregate data.

Do not invent villains. The opposing force may be a system, limit, market, rule, or incompatible good.

### Scope boundary

Write exclusions explicitly:

```text
This article will not attempt to evaluate every AI meeting product,
cover all privacy law, or measure total industry revenue.
```

Scope is a quality control tool, not an apology.

## Three working templates

### Emerging story

```text
[Actor/change] is beginning to [development], which is already changing
[specific group/system] through [direct impact].
```

### Mature story

```text
Although [development] is already known, new evidence shows [underreported impact].
As [affected group] responds by [reaction], [opposing actor/constraint] is creating
[conflict or second-order effect].
```

### Profile

```text
[Person] is defined not by résumé facts but by [2–3 traits/tensions].
Those qualities become visible when [person] takes [actions] under [constraint].
```

## Hypothesis stress test

Score each item 0–2.

| Test | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Change | static topic | implied change | clear actor + verb |
| Impact | abstract | plausible | concrete and evidenced |
| Human center | none | illustrative only | causally relevant |
| Reaction | none | opinion | observable action |
| Conflict | generic | one-sided | real competing forces |
| Verifiability | mostly judgment | partly testable | clauses map to evidence |
| Scope | unlimited | vague boundary | explicit exclusions |
| Disconfirmability | cannot fail | weakly revisable | clear evidence could change it |

A score below 10/16 usually means the story needs more reporting or narrower scope.

## Competing-hypothesis method

When several angles are plausible, create 2–4 candidates and compare:

| Candidate | Importance | Evidence access | Human action | Originality | Fairness risk | Feasibility |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| A | 5 | 4 | 5 | 3 | 2 | 4 |
| B | 4 | 2 | 3 | 5 | 4 | 2 |

Do not choose only by novelty. A less novel angle with strong direct evidence can produce the better article.

## Revision checkpoints

Rewrite the hypothesis:

- after the landscape research pass;
- after the first direct-participant interview;
- when decisive counterevidence appears;
- before outlining;
- after the first full draft.

Keep earlier versions in the dossier so the reporting logic remains visible.
